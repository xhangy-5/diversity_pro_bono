Gender Diversity in Research: Dashboard Indicators

 

Inter & Multidisciplinary research indicators

Interdisciplinary research combines knowledge or methods from two or more academic disciplines into one activity (e.g., a research project). Interdisciplinary research is becoming increasingly important as many of the most pressing problems facing society today require solutions that draw on knowledge from multiple disciplines.[1] To measure interdisciplinarity and multidisciplinarity, we use as proxies the disciplinary diversity of authors (DDA) and disciplinary diversity of references (DDR).

  • Share of highly interdisciplinary publications: This measures the extent to which research publications cite sources from different disciplines, to reflect the diversity of knowledge that is being integrated into the publication (disciplinary diversity of references – DDR). It is computed based on the material cited by the publication. The indicator considers (a) the number of different subfields that are being cited, (b) the distribution of those citations across the cited subfields, and (c) the intellectual proximity of those subfields to one another. Share of highly interdisciplinary publications refers to the share of an entity’s papers among the top 10% with the highest DDR in the world.

Examples of public reports and publications for this indicator:

  • 2022: Do women undertake interdisciplinary research more than men, and do self-citations bias observed differences? | Quantitative Science Studies (MIT.edu)
  • 2021: Evaluation of the Belmont Forum (belmontforum.org)

 

  • Share of highly multidisciplinary publications: This measures the extent to which authors from different disciplines collaborate on research projects. It reflects the diversity of the prior disciplinary backgrounds of a paper’s co-authors and is calculated to account for the number of distinct disciplines, the cognitive distance that separates them, and the balance between them. A paper co-authored by authors whose previous papers were distributed across subfields of science in a similar pattern would score lower than papers bringing together authors with different backgrounds even if those authors, individually, have published in a less diverse set of subfields. Share of highly multidisciplinary (with high disciplinary diversity of authors - DDA) publications refers to the share of an entity’s papers among the top 10% with the highest DDA in the world.

Examples of public reports and publications for this indicator:

  • 2022: Do women undertake interdisciplinary research more than men, and do self-citations bias observed differences? | Quantitative Science Studies (edu)
  • 2021: Evaluation of the Belmont Forum (belmontforum.org)

 

Open Access indicators

Share of publications available in open access is the percentage of research outputs that are available online, free of access charges or other barriers. Open access can be applied to all forms of published research output, including peer-reviewed journal articles and conference papers. It is computed for OA overall as well as by type of open access, using data from Unpaywall.

There are different models of open access (OA) publishing:

  • In Hybrid and Gold OA, the publisher makes all articles and related content freely available to read at the time of publication.
  • Green OA articles are available in an OA repository, independently from publication by a publisher. Note that Green OA may be affected by the “embargo effect” because many journal publishers do not allow authors to post their publication in an OA repository until a certain amount of time has gone by. This effect usually exerts its strongest influence on the two most recent publication years.
  • Bronze OA refers to articles that are freely available but do not have a clearly identifiable license.

OA color for this indicator is defined according to the best OA location where a document is retrieved. For more details about how OA colors are defined, please refer to the Unpaywall definitions.

Examples of public reports where this indicator has been used:

  • 2022: Investigating the effects of open sharing commitments | Reports (Wellcome.org), and Assessing the evolution of open science practices: the Wellcome Joint Statement on COVID-19 (Science-Metrix.com)
  • 2020: Achieving an equitable transition to open access for researchers in lower and middle-income countries (Elsevier.com)

 

Research Impact Indicators

  • Field-weighted citation impact (FWCI) is a measure of citation impact that normalizes the citations received by an article against the world benchmark of citations received in the same field, publication type, and year of publication. The World FWCI is indexed to a value of 1.0, meaning that values above 1.0 indicate above average citation impact. For example, a value of 1.7 indicates a citation impact that is 1.7 times the average or 70% above average.

Examples of public reports and publications where this indicator has been used or described:

  • 2023: G20-report-June-2023.pdf (elsevier.com) Fostering collaboration: a study of scientific publications with authors in G20 countries
  • Updated 2022: What is Field-weighted Citation Impact (FWCI)? (elsevier.com)

 

  • Share of publications cited by patents informs on the relative uptake of scientific research into innovation. It reflects the number of scientific publications cited by a patent proportional to the total number of scientific publications. It is measured by linking records in Scopus to the patent literature. Patent applications filed at the USPTO, the European Patent Office (EPO) and the and through the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) are considered.

The patent literature integrates the scientific literature at a very slow pace, particularly because it can take many years to develop and patent inventions. For this reason, patent citation scores for the most recent years examined are low, because citations have not had time to accrue.

Examples of public reports and publications for this indicator:

  • 2022: Research trends and impact report on the field of Aggregation Induced Emission AIE (elsevier.com)

 

  • Share of publications cited by policy documents is an indicator that informs on the uptake of scientific discovery by policymakers, governmental bodies and agencies. This is the number of scientific publications cited by a policy-related document proportional to the total number of scientific publications. It is measured by linking records in Scopus to the policy document literature. The data source of policy documents is Overton, which is the world’s largest searchable index of policy documents, guidelines, think-tank publications and working papers.

Like patent literature, policy literature integrates scientific knowledge at a slow pace, and it can take many years for citations to accrue. For this reason, policy citation scores for the most recent years reported are an undercount.

Examples of public reports and publications for this indicator:

  • 2022: Tracking the impact of research on education policy during the COVID-19 pandemic (ucl.ac.uk)
  • 2022: Overton-A-bibliometric-database-of-policy-document (mit.edu)
  • 2022: How relevant is climate change research for climate change policy? An empirical analysis based on Overton data (jplos.org)
  • 2021: Evaluation of the Belmont Forum (belmontforum.org)

 

  • Altmetrics (alternative metrics) assess the uptake of scientific literature by popular culture, such as news and online content. Altmetrics are measured by linking records in Scopus to PlumX, the data source of journalistic and trade news documents, Wikipedia mentions, and blog mentions. PlumX maintains a database recording the uptake of scientific outputs beyond the scientific literature in, for example, social media, blogs, news, and educational resources.

Examples of public reports and publications for this indicator:

  • About PlumX Metrics - Plum Analytics

 

Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) indicators

  • Share of publications thematically aligned with a certain SDG: The United Nations’ Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) challenge the global community to build a world where no one is left behind. They are increasingly recognized as objectives against which to measure the societal outcomes of research efforts. Elsevier has built thematic data sets covering research related to each of the SDGs and can measure the shares of given publication sets that fall within each SDG. (Further details on the Elsevier 2023 mapping are available here.)This measurement provides a sense of the probability to which a publication set could potentially be thematically related and useful to advancing an SDG.

Examples of public reports where this indicator has been used:

  • 2023: Fostering collaboration: a study of scientific publications with authors in G20 countries
  • 2022: Wales SDG Report (elsevier.com):
  • 2022: Report: How can research help the world hit net zero by 2050? (elsevier.com)
  • 2022: Sustainability through a gender lens: The extent to which research on UN Sustainable Development Goals includes sex and gender consideration (plos.org)

 

Authorship indicator and gender inference

Share of author count by gender is the share of authors who are women or men. To infer a gender from an author’s name, Elsevier employs an approach that combines the use of existing and established name/gender lists and the use of Namsor, designed to determine the gender of names, taking into account different elements such as given name, surname, ethnicity and country. The indicator has known limitations for Asian names. In this dashboard we have limited the data to those countries with sufficient numbers of researchers, and a sufficient quality for inferred gender. For more details refer to the report.

For a discussion of this approach and its limitations, see: 2018: science-metrix_bibliometric_indicators_womens_contribution_to_science_report.pdf (Science-Metrix.com)

Examples of public reports and publications for this indicator:

  • 2022: Imperfect Inferences: A Practical Assessment (acm.org)
  • 2022: Do women undertake interdisciplinary research more than men, and do self-citations bias observed differences? | Quantitative Science Studies (edu) & How to encourage gender equity in interdisciplinary research (timeshighereducation.com)
  • 2022: OECD dashboard on women in AI (OECD)
  • 2021: Elsevier's reports on gender in research (Elsevier.com)

Subject areas and subfields included in the analysis:

Titles in Scopus are classified under four broad subject clusters (life sciences, physical sciences, health sciences and social sciences & humanities), which are further divided into 27 major subject areas (known as All Science Journal Classification or ASJC). Titles may belong to more than one subject area. Subject areas in this report are based on 26 subject areas, with titles classified as “Multidisciplinary” by ASJC reclassified for this report.

To define subfields of research within medicine, we leveraged a system in which the major subfields of medicine are further classified into subcategories. Because there is considerable overlap in some subcategories, we defined medicine subfields by grouping the appropriate subcategories based on the frequency that publications were categorized in overlapping subcategories. For example, we created the subfield “Fertility & Birth” because a high percentage of publications in the subcategory “Obstetrics and Gynecology” were also classified in the subcategory “Reproductive Medicine”.
