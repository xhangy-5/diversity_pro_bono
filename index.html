Gender Diversity in Research: Dashboard Indicators 

Inter & Multidisciplinary research indicators 

Interdisciplinary research combines knowledge or methods from two or more academic disciplines into one activity (e.g., a research project). Interdisciplinary research is becoming increasingly important as many of the most pressing problems facing society today require solutions that draw on knowledge from multiple disciplines. To measure interdisciplinarity and multidisciplinarity, we use as proxies the disciplinary diversity of authors (DDA) and disciplinary diversity of references (DDR). 

  • Share of highly DDA (highly multidisciplinary) publications: This measures the extent to which authors from different disciplines collaborate on research projects. It reflects the diversity of the prior disciplinary backgrounds of a paper’s co-authors and is calculated to account for the number of distinct disciplines, the cognitive distance that separates them, and the balance between them. A paper co-authored by authors whose previous papers were distributed across subfields of science in a similar pattern would score lower than papers bringing together authors with different backgrounds even if those authors, individually, have published in a less diverse set of subfields. Share of highly DDA (highly multidisciplinary) publications refers to the share of an entity’s papers among the top 10% with the highest DDA in the world. 

Examples of public reports and publications for this indicator: 

  • 2022: Do women undertake interdisciplinary research more than men, and do self-citations bias observed differences? | Quantitative Science Studies (MIT.edu) 

  • 2021: Evaluation of the Belmont Forum (belmontforum.org)  

  • Share of highly DDR (highly interdisciplinary) publications: A publication’s disciplinary diversity of references measures the extent to which research publications cite sources from different disciplines, to reflect the diversity of knowledge that is being integrated into the publication. It is computed based on the material cited by the publication. The indicator considers (a) the number of different subfields that are being cited, (b) the distribution of those citations across the cited subfields, and (c) the intellectual proximity of those subfields to one another. Share of highly DDR (highly interdisciplinary) publications refers to the share of an entity’s papers among the top 10% with the highest DDR in the world. 

Examples of public reports and publications for this indicator: 

  • 2022: Do women undertake interdisciplinary research more than men, and do self-citations bias observed differences? | Quantitative Science Studies (MIT.edu) 

  • 2021: Evaluation of the Belmont Forum (belmontforum.org)  

Open Access indicators 

Share of publications available in open access is the percentage of research outputs that are available online, free of access charges or other barriers. Open access can be applied to all forms of published research output, including peer-reviewed journal articles and conference papers. It is computed for OA overall as well as by type of open access, using data from Unpaywall.  

There are different models of open access (OA) publishing: 

  • In Hybrid and Gold OA, the publisher makes all articles and related content freely available to read at the time of publication.  

  • Green OA articles are available in an OA repository, independently from publication by a publisher. Note that Green OA may be affected by the “embargo effect” because many journal publishers do not allow authors to post their publication in an OA repository until a certain amount of time has gone by. This effect usually exerts its strongest influence on the two most recent publication years. 

  • Bronze OA refers to articles that are freely available but do not have a clearly identifiable license. 

OA color for this indicator is defined according to the best OA location where a document is retrieved. For more details about how OA colors are defined, please refer to the Unpaywall definitions.  

Examples of public reports where this indicator has been used: 

  • 2022: Investigating the effects of open sharing commitments | Reports (Wellcome.org), and Assessing the evolution of open science practices: the Wellcome Joint Statement on COVID-19 (Science-Metrix.com) 

  • 2020: Achieving an equitable transition to open access for researchers in lower and middle-income countries (Elsevier.com) 

Research Impact Indicators 

  • Field-weighted citation impact (FWCI) is a measure of citation impact that normalizes the citations received by an article against the world benchmark of citations received in the same field, publication type, and year of publication. The World FWCI is indexed to a value of 1.0, meaning that values above 1.0 indicate above average citation impact. For example, a value of 1.7 indicates a citation impact that is 1.7 times the average or 70% above average. 

Examples of public reports and publications where this indicator has been used or described: 

  • 2023: G20-report-June-2023.pdf (elsevier.com) 

  • Updated 2022: What is Field-weighted Citation Impact (FWCI)? (elsevier.com) 

  • 2019: AS Alzheimer Report (elsevier.com), and many other reports published by Elsevier Analytical Services.  

  • Patent citation score informs on the relative uptake of scientific research into innovation. It reflects the number of scientific publications cited by a patent proportional to the total number of scientific publications. It is measured by linking records in Scopus to the patent literature. Patent applications filed at the USPTO, the European Patent Office (EPO) and the and through the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) are considered.  

The patent literature integrates the scientific literature at a very slow pace, particularly because it can take many years to develop and patent inventions. For this reason, patent citation scores for the most recent years examined are low, because citations have not had time to accrue.  

Examples of public reports and publications for this indicator: 

  • 2022: Research trends and impact report on the field of Aggregation Induced Emission AIE (in Chinese) (elsevier.com) 

  • 2021: Comparative research report of 20 global cities (elsevier.com) 

  • Policy citation score is an indicator that informs on the uptake of scientific discovery by policymakers, governmental bodies and agencies. This is the number of scientific publications cited by a policy-related document proportional to the total number of scientific publications. It is measured by linking records in Scopus to the policy document literature. The data source of policy documents is Overton, which is the world’s largest searchable index of policy documents, guidelines, think-tank publications and working papers. 

Like patent literature, policy literature integrates scientific knowledge at a slow pace, and it can take many years for citations to accrue. For this reason, policy citation scores for the most recent years reported are an undercount. 

Examples of public reports and publications for this indicator: 

  • 2022: Tracking the impact of research on education policy during the COVID-19 pandemic (ucl.ac.uk) 

  • 2022: Overton-A-bibliometric-database-of-policy-document (mit.edu) 

  • 2022: How relevant is climate change research for climate change policy? An empirical analysis based on Overton data (journals.plos.org) 

  • 2021: Evaluation of the Belmont Forum (belmontforum.org)  

  • Altmetrics (alternative metrics) assess the uptake of scientific literature by popular culture, such as news and online content. Altmetrics are measured by linking records in Scopus to PlumX, the data source of journalistic and trade news documents, Wikipedia mentions, and blog mentions. PlumX maintains a database recording the uptake of scientific outputs beyond the scientific literature in, for example, social media, blogs, news, and educational resources. 

Examples of public reports and publications for this indicator: 

  • About PlumX Metrics - Plum Analytics

Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) indicators 

  • Share of publications thematically aligned with a certain SDG: The United Nations’ Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) challenge the global community to build a world where no one is left behind. They are increasingly recognized as objectives against which to measure the societal outcomes of research efforts. Elsevier has built thematic data sets covering research related to each of the SDGs and can measure the shares of given publication sets that fall within each SDG. This measurement provides a sense of the probability to which a publication set could potentially be thematically related and useful to advancing an SDG. 

Examples of public reports where this indicator has been used: 

  • 2023: G20-report-June-2023 (elsevier.com) 

  • 2022: Wales SDG Report (elsevier.com) 

  • 2022: Report: How can research help the world hit net zero by 2050? (elsevier.com) 

  • 2022: Amsterdam 2022 Report (elsevier.com) 

  • 2022: Sustainability through a gender lens: The extent to which research on UN Sustainable Development Goals includes sex and gender consideration (plos.org) 

Authorship indicator and gender inference 

  • Share of author count by gender is the share of authors who are women or men. To infer a gender from an author’s name, Elsevier employs an approach that combines the use of existing and established name/gender lists and the use of Namsor, designed to determine the gender of names, taking into account different elements such as given name, surname, ethnicity and country. The indicator has known limitations for Asian names.  

For a discussion of this approach and its limitations, see: 2018: science-metrix_bibliometric_indicators_womens_contribution_to_science_report.pdf (Science-Metrix.com)
